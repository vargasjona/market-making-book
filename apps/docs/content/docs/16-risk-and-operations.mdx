### The non-negotiables

<ul> <li><strong>Hard inventory limits</strong> — per market and aggregate, enforced in the engine (GLFT does it natively: hit the bound, stop quoting that side). A limit you can override under stress is not a limit.</li> <li><strong>Kill switches</strong> — automatic full-cancel + halt on: max drawdown, inventory breach, stale market data (heartbeat timeout), abnormal order-to-fill latency, or connectivity loss. On Kalshi, set <code>cancel_order_on_pause</code>; everywhere, know what happens to your resting orders when your process dies. Dead-man's-switch first, dashboard second.</li> <li><strong>Markout monitoring</strong> — your single most informative live metric (Ch. 5): price drift at +1s/+5s/+30s after your fills, by market, by side, by hour. Negative beyond half-spread = you're the exit liquidity; widen or leave.</li> <li><strong>Event discipline</strong> — a calendar of scheduled releases (macro prints, earnings) and a real-time jump detector for unscheduled ones; both trigger the same reflex: widen/pull, reprice, re-tighten. In sports this fires on <em>every scoring event</em>.</li> <li><strong>Fee accounting at trade granularity</strong> — the viability inequality (Ch. 7) audited daily. Many "profitable" MM books are profitable only before fees or only because of rebates; know which business you're actually in.</li> </ul>

Slide the limit and watch the trade-off: too tight and you sit out good periods; too loose and a single toxic hour erases a month.</p>

### The triple barrier: three exits, decided in advance

<p>The kill switch protects the whole book; the <strong>triple barrier</strong> protects each position. The idea (popularized by López de Prado, and the risk backbone of the live crypto experiments in Chapter 17): the moment a position opens, three exits are armed — a <strong>take-profit</strong> price above, a <strong>stop-loss</strong> price below, and a <strong>time limit</strong>. Whichever the price path touches first closes the trade. No debate, no "let me watch it a bit longer" — the exit was chosen before the entry, which is the entire point.</p>

### Sizing and capital

<p>Size positions so the worst credible jump against a full inventory is an acceptable day, not an account event. In binaries the worst case is explicit: a full bound position moving to 0 or 1. In perps, include funding flips and liquidation cascades (JELLY, Ch. 11). Keep idle capital productive where the venue pays for it (Kalshi interest) but never let yield-seeking dictate position structure.</p>

### Regulatory hygiene

<ul> <li><strong>Kalshi:</strong> CFTC-regulated; cleanest US-legal path for event contracts.</li> <li><strong>Polymarket / Hyperliquid:</strong> smart-contract, oracle, and jurisdiction risk are part of the position. Self-custody discipline applies.</li> <li><strong>US equities:</strong> liquidity provision yes, dealer-like patterns at scale can trigger registration questions (Ch. 13). Know the line before approaching it.</li> <li><strong>Quoting conduct:</strong> spoofing/layering prohibitions apply to bots as to humans; your quoting logic should never place orders it intends not to honor.</li> </ul>
