### Japanese candlesticks: four numbers, one story

<p>Charts don't plot every trade — they <strong>compress</strong> them. A <strong>candlestick</strong> summarizes all trading inside a time window with four numbers, <strong>OHLC</strong>:</p>

<ul> <li><strong>Open</strong> — the first trade price of the window;</li> <li><strong>High / Low</strong> — the extremes reached (drawn as the thin <em>wicks</em> or shadows);</li> <li><strong>Close</strong> — the last trade price. The thick <em>body</em> spans open→close: <span style={{color:'var(--bid)',fontWeight:'600'}}>green/bullish</span> when close &gt; open, <span style={{color:'var(--ask)',fontWeight:'600'}}>red/bearish</span> when close &lt; open.</li> </ul>

The wicks mark the extremes the price visited and abandoned. Right: the three shapes every platform trader names on sight — read them as flow summaries, not prophecies: a <strong>doji</strong> is a fought draw, a <strong>hammer</strong> is a rejected sell-off, an <strong>engulfing</strong> candle is one side taking control.</div> </div>

<div className="parable"><span className="tag">Parable · The rice merchant of Sakata</span> In 1750s Osaka, rice trader Munehisa Homma grew rich at the Dōjima Exchange — the world's first organized futures market — and recorded prices as opens, highs, lows and closes, reading the "battle between buyers and sellers" in each day's shape. His notation became the candlestick chart, popularized in the West only in the 1990s by Steve Nison. Two and a half centuries later, every platform from Binance to Kalshi still renders markets in the script of an Edo-period rice merchant.</div>

<p>Traders name recurring candle shapes — a <strong>doji</strong> (open ≈ close: a tug-of-war that ended in a draw), a <strong>hammer</strong> (long lower wick: sellers pushed down, buyers slammed it back), an <strong>engulfing</strong> candle (a body that swallows the previous one: one side just took control). Read these as <em>descriptions of order flow</em>, not prophecies — academic evidence for candlestick patterns as standalone predictors is weak. Their real value is vocabulary: each shape summarizes who was hitting bids and lifting asks inside the window.</p>

<div className="keyidea"><span className="tag">The market maker's reading of a candle</span>A candle is the shadow that thousands of individual fills cast on the chart — and the market maker was the counterparty inside it. The wicks are where takers paid the most for immediacy (and where stale quotes got picked off); the body is the net drift the MM's inventory had to survive; the volume bar is how many times the spread was paid. Chartists read candles to predict; market makers read them to remember.</div>

### Timeframes: one market, many movies

<p>The same tick stream renders into 1-minute, 1-hour, or daily candles — and the three movies can disagree: a brutal downtrend on the 5-minute chart can be an invisible blip on the daily. Platform traders practice <strong>multi-timeframe analysis</strong>: establish regime and trend on a higher timeframe, time entries on a lower one. Two facts matter mathematically:</p>

<ul> <li><strong>Volatility scales with the square root of time.</strong> Under diffusion, the standard deviation over a window of length τ grows like σ·√τ — a 1% hourly vol is roughly a 4.9% daily vol (√24 ≈ 4.9). This is the same σ that will price your spread in Chapter 6; choosing the estimation window <em>is</em> choosing a timeframe.</li> <li><strong>Aggregation hides microstructure.</strong> A candle cannot show queue position, book imbalance, or who was maker and who was taker. Everything in Chapter 7 lives <em>below</em> the lowest timeframe your platform will draw.</li> </ul>

<p>The market maker's "timeframe" is the tick — but regime detection (is this a calm range or a trending, toxic hour?) is genuinely a higher-timeframe question, and that is exactly where the platform toolkit re-enters the MM's engine (layer L3 of Proposal F, Chapter 18).</p>

### Moving averages: the EMA you'll never stop using

<p>A <strong>simple moving average (SMA)</strong> is the mean of the last N closes. An <strong>exponential moving average (EMA)</strong> weights recent prices more, via one cheap recursion:</p>

<div className="eq"><span className="lbl">EMA recursion (α = 2/(N+1))</span> EMA<sub>t</sub> = α · <i>P</i><sub>t</sub> + (1 − α) · EMA<sub>t−1</sub> <span className="where"><span className="wt">What the symbols mean</span> <span className="sym">EMA<sub>t</sub></span> — the average's new value, right now &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym"><i>P</i><sub>t</sub></span> — the latest price (the newest close) &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">EMA<sub>t−1</sub></span> — the average's previous value, one candle ago &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">α</span> (alpha) — the weight given to the newest price, a number between 0 and 1; it comes from the window length <span className="sym">N</span> (e.g. N = 19 → α = 0.1, so each new price contributes 10% and the old average keeps 90%). In words: <em>new average = a little bit of the newest price + mostly the old average.</em></span></div>

<p>Directional traders use crossovers — fast EMA crossing above a slow one (the 50/200-day version is the famous <strong>golden cross</strong>) — as trend signals. The market maker uses the same recursion for something deeper: it is an <strong>online filter</strong> that needs no history buffer, just one stored value. Inside a production quoting engine, EWMA (the same formula) estimates realized volatility from squared returns (the RiskMetrics tradition), smooths the fair-value anchor, tracks average fill rates and funding. When Chapter 6's formulas ask for σ, an EWMA of squared mid-price returns is usually what supplies it.</p>

<p>The difference between SMA and EMA is invisible in a formula and obvious in a picture. Hit the price with a sudden step and watch who reacts first:</p>

### Volume, VWAP, and the flow you want

<p>Volume confirms what price claims: a move on heavy volume reflects broad repricing; the same move on thin volume is often just a hole in the book. The <strong>VWAP</strong> (volume-weighted average price), Σ(p·v)/Σv over the session, is the institutional benchmark: execution desks slice large parent orders into child orders across the day (VWAP/TWAP algos) precisely to avoid moving the market. Hear that as a market maker: <em>schedule-driven institutional flow is uninformed at the tick scale</em> — it trades because the clock says so, not because of what happens in the next minute. It is among the most benign flow you will ever be filled by, the polar opposite of Chapter 5's informed traders.</p>

### Support, resistance, and bands — microstructure in disguise

<ul> <li><strong>Support/resistance levels</strong> are real, but not mystical: they are prices where resting limit orders cluster (visible in the book as depth walls) and where stop orders accumulate just beyond. Breaching a level detonates those stops — stops are market orders waiting to be born — which is why "breakouts" accelerate and why an MM's jump detector should treat round numbers and prior highs with suspicion.</li> <li><strong>Bollinger Bands</strong> draw mean ± k·σ around an SMA. Look familiar? It is structurally the same object as the Avellaneda–Stoikov quote pair around a reservation price (Chapter 6) — a volatility-scaled envelope. The chartist trades the band; the maker <em>is</em> the band.</li> <li><strong>RSI</strong> and other oscillators measure short-horizon momentum vs. mean-reversion. The regime they sniff is existential for an MM: <strong>mean-reverting chop is the MM's paradise</strong> (both quotes fill repeatedly, inventory self-corrects), while <strong>trend is the MM's tax</strong> (one side fills relentlessly and inventory piles up against the move — the exact failure of naive quoting and grid bots, Chapter 15).</li> </ul>

<div className="warn"><span className="tag">Honest framing</span>Every indicator is a transformation of past price and volume — it contains no information the tape didn't already have, and most have near-zero standalone predictive power after costs. Use them as <em>state descriptors</em> (what regime am I in?) rather than <em>signals</em> (what happens next?). Anything you do trade on, backtest first — Chapter 18's proposals all ship with a testing plan for exactly this reason.</div>
