### Fear #1 — Inventory risk (Ho &amp; Stoll, 1981)

<p>Ho and Stoll gave the first rigorous treatment of Rosa's first fear: a risk-averse dealer facing random buy and sell arrivals should not quote symmetrically around fair value once they hold a position. Holding inventory exposes the dealer to price moves they have no opinion about; a rational dealer charges for that exposure by <strong>skewing</strong> quotes — long inventory pushes both bid and ask <em>down</em> (eager to sell, reluctant to buy more), short inventory pushes both <em>up</em>. The skew grows with risk aversion, variance, and the time you expect to hold the position. Avellaneda–Stoikov (Chapter 6) turned this insight into the closed-form equations everyone uses today.</p>

### Fear #2 — Adverse selection (Glosten &amp; Milgrom, 1985)

<p>Glosten and Milgrom proved something stranger: <strong>even a risk-neutral market maker with zero costs and zero profit must quote a positive spread</strong>, purely because of information asymmetry. In their model, each arriving trader is <em>informed</em> with probability μ (they know the asset's true value) or a <em>noise trader</em> with probability 1−μ (they trade for liquidity reasons). The MM can't tell them apart. But the MM knows that a buy order is more likely to come from someone who knows the value is high — so the very act of receiving a buy order should raise the MM's estimate of value. The zero-profit quotes are conditional expectations:</p>

<div className="eq"><span className="lbl">Glosten–Milgrom quotes</span> ask = E[ <i>V</i> | next trader buys ]&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;bid = E[ <i>V</i> | next trader sells ] <span className="where"><span className="wt">What the symbols mean</span> <span className="sym"><i>V</i></span> — the asset's true (unknown) value &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">E[ <i>V</i> | … ]</span> — read the bar "|" as <em>"given that"</em>: the average value of V <em>given that</em> the event after the bar happens. So the ask is: "what is the asset worth, on average, in the worlds where someone chooses to buy from me?" Since buyers are disproportionately people who know V is high, that conditional average sits <em>above</em> the plain average — and that's the ask.</span></div>

<p>The ask sits above the unconditional expectation and the bid below it; the gap is the pure <strong>adverse-selection spread</strong>. Each trade then updates the MM's beliefs (Bayes' rule), which is precisely how prices come to reflect information over time.</p>

<div className="parable"><span className="tag">Parable · The used-car dealer</span> A used-car dealer offers to buy any car, sight unseen. Who shows up first? Disproportionately, people whose cars have hidden problems. The dealer isn't quoting against the <em>average</em> car — they're quoting against the cars that <em>choose to come</em>. So the buy price must be lowered below the average value until the deal breaks even against the self-selected flow. That discount is adverse selection, and it exists even if the dealer fears no risk at all.</div>

### Glosten–Milgrom by the numbers

<p>The model becomes unforgettable with one concrete case. Take a binary contract (a Kalshi-style YES) whose true value is $1 or $0 with equal prior probability. A fraction <strong>μ</strong> of arriving traders are informed (they know the answer); the rest flip a coin. Bayes' rule gives the zero-profit quotes in closed form — and at a 50/50 prior they collapse to something beautiful:</p>

<div className="eq"><span className="lbl">GM quotes at a 50¢ prior</span> ask = (1 + μ)/2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; bid = (1 − μ)/2 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; spread = μ <span className="where"><span className="wt">What the symbols mean</span> <span className="sym">μ</span> (mu) — the fraction of arriving traders who are <em>informed</em> (they already know the outcome), from 0 to 1. Prices are in dollars on a contract that settles at $1 or $0. Example: μ = 0.2 → ask = (1+0.2)/2 = 60¢, bid = (1−0.2)/2 = 40¢, spread = 20¢.</span></div>

<p><strong>The spread equals the informed fraction.</strong> If 20% of your counterparties know the outcome, the break-even market is 40¢ bid / 60¢ ask — a 20¢ spread on a 50¢ contract, with zero risk aversion and zero fees. And the model doesn't stop at one trade: after each fill the market maker updates the prior (a buy moves it up to the old ask, a sell down to the old bid) and posts fresh quotes around the new belief. Run it yourself:</p>

### Kyle (1985): how much does flow move price?

<p>Kyle modeled a single informed trader hiding inside noise-trader flow, with a market maker setting price as a linear function of the net order flow:</p>

<div className="eq"><span className="lbl">Kyle's lambda</span>Δ<i>P</i> = λ · (net order flow) <span className="where"><span className="wt">What the symbols mean</span> <span className="sym">Δ<i>P</i></span> — the change in price (Δ, delta, always means "change in") &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">λ</span> (lambda) — price impact per unit of flow: how many cents the price moves for each extra contract of one-sided pressure &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">net order flow</span> — buy volume minus sell volume over the window. A small λ means a deep market that absorbs flow; a big λ means a shallow one where every trade moves the price.</span></div>

<p><strong>λ (lambda)</strong> is the price impact per unit of flow — the inverse of market depth. Kyle showed λ rises with the variance of the true value and falls with the amount of noise trading: <em>the more uninformed flow there is to hide in, the deeper the market</em>. For a practitioner, λ is something to estimate continuously: when your fills start predicting price moves (your post-fill markouts go negative), the flow has turned toxic and your quotes are too tight.</p>

<p>The simulation below makes adverse selection visceral. Noise traders hit your quotes at random — you collect the spread. Informed traders only hit you <em>just before the price jumps in their favor</em> — every informed fill is money out. Slide μ and watch the business die.</p>

<div className="keyidea"><span className="tag">The operational test</span>Measure your <strong>post-fill markout</strong>: the price change a few seconds after each of your fills. If markouts are systematically negative and bigger than your half-spread, adverse selection is eating you. No spread you can quote fixes informed flow — you fix it by quoting wider at dangerous moments, or not at all.</div>
