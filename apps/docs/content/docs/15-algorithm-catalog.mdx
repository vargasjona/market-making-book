<div className="algo"><h4>1 · Naive symmetric quoting</h4><div className="fit">FIT: teaching baseline only</div> <p>Quote mid ± δ with fixed δ, forever. The fruit vendor who never adjusts. Every weakness this book has discussed, in one strategy: inventory random-walks unbounded, adverse selection unpriced, fees unconsidered. You already watched it bleed — the Chapter 3 simulator runs exactly this strategy.</p> <div className="proscons"><div className="p">+ Trivial; the control group for every backtest you run.</div><div className="c">− Run over by trends and informed flow; bleeds in jumps.</div></div></div>

<div className="algo"><h4>2 · Avellaneda–Stoikov</h4><div className="fit">FIT: liquid, diffusive markets (crypto majors, equities)</div> <p>The canonical inventory-aware quoter, derived gently in Chapter 6. Its whole logic compresses into two lines:</p> <div className="eq"><span className="lbl">The AS engine in two lines</span> <i>r</i> = <i>s</i> − <i>q</i>·γ·σ²·(<i>T</i>−<i>t</i>) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; δ<sub>total</sub> = γ·σ²·(<i>T</i>−<i>t</i>) + (2/γ)·ln(1+γ/k) <span className="where"><span className="wt">What the symbols mean</span> All symbols exactly as in Chapter 6: <span className="sym"><i>r</i></span> — your private fair value, shifted off the mid <span className="sym"><i>s</i></span> by inventory <span className="sym"><i>q</i></span> &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">γ</span> — risk aversion &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">σ²</span> — price variance &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym"><i>T</i>−<i>t</i></span> — time left &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">k</span> — how fast fills die off with distance. Quotes go at <span className="sym">r ± δ/2</span>. In words: <em>center on what the position makes it worth to you, and open the spread with risk and close it with competition.</em></span></div> <p>Hummingbot ships a ready-made lab for parameter intuition: the legacy <code>avellaneda_market_making</code> strategy exposes <code>risk_factor</code> (γ) and an order-shape factor (η), and — since a 2022 rework — <em>estimates</em> k and σ live from trading intensity and volatility buffers rather than asking you for them. (Hummingbot now recommends its V2 controllers — <code>pmm_simple</code> / <code>pmm_dynamic</code> — for new bots; the AS math lives on inside them.)</p> <div className="proscons"><div className="p">+ Principled, closed-form, the industry's shared language.</div><div className="c">− Brownian assumption; no adverse-selection term; unbounded inventory; constant parameters.</div></div></div>

<div className="algo"><h4>3 · Guéant–Lehalle–Fernandez-Tapia (GLFT)</h4><div className="fit">FIT: production AS — anywhere AS fits, with real risk limits</div> <p>AS re-solved with hard inventory bounds |q| ≤ Q (Q = the most contracts you allow yourself to hold); the mathematics collapses to simple equations with cheap closed-form approximations. The behavioral difference is easiest to <em>see</em>:</p> <div className="fig"> <div className="fig-bar"><span className="dot d1"></span><span className="dot d2"></span><span className="dot d3"></span><span className="fig-t">interactive — AS vs GLFT: what a hard inventory bound does</span></div> <div className="fig-body"> <canvas id="cv-glft" height="220"></canvas> <div className="ctrl-row"> <div className="ctrl"><label>Your inventory q: <b id="gl2-q-v">0</b></label><input type="range" id="gl2-q" min="-12" max="12" defaultValue="0" /></div> <div className="ctrl"><label>Inventory bound Q: <b id="gl2-Q-v">8</b></label><input type="range" id="gl2-Q" min="2" max="12" defaultValue="8" /></div> </div> <div className="readout"> <div className="r"><span className="k">AS quotes</span><span className="v" id="gl2-as">—</span></div> <div className="r"><span className="k">GLFT quotes</span><span className="v" id="gl2-gl">—</span></div> <div className="r"><span className="k">GLFT status</span><span className="v" id="gl2-st" style={{fontSize:'13px',maxWidth:'300px'}}>—</span></div> </div> </div> <div className="fig-cap">Drag q toward the bound Q. Both engines skew their quotes down as you get longer (blue = AS, amber = GLFT) — but AS keeps bidding forever, letting inventory run past any limit, while GLFT's bid simply <strong>switches off</strong> at q = +Q (and its ask switches off at q = −Q). One side dark = the algorithm refusing to dig the hole deeper. That refusal is the entire practical difference — and why GLFT is the version you deploy.</div> </div> <div className="proscons"><div className="p">+ Bounded risk; stable; computationally trivial; extends to multi-asset.</div><div className="c">− Still parametric and diffusion-based; calibration drift remains your problem.</div></div></div>

<div className="algo"><h4>4 · Grid trading</h4><div className="fit">FIT: ranging crypto; explicitly NOT trends</div> <p>A ladder of buys below and sells above a center price. When price dips a rung, you buy; when it climbs back, you sell — each completed round trip banks one rung:</p> <div className="eq"><span className="lbl">Profit per completed rung</span> rung P&amp;L = grid step − 2·fees <span className="where"><span className="wt">What the symbols mean</span> <span className="sym">grid step</span> — the fixed price distance between neighboring rungs of the ladder (e.g. one rung every 50¢) &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">2·fees</span> — you pay the venue twice per round trip, once for the buy and once for the sell. A vending machine for mean reversion — no model of anything. The catch is what happens when price <em>doesn't</em> come back:</span></div> <div className="fig"> <div className="fig-bar"><span className="dot d1"></span><span className="dot d2"></span><span className="dot d3"></span><span className="fig-t">simulation — the grid: vending machine or falling knife</span></div> <div className="fig-body"> <canvas id="cv-grid" height="240"></canvas> <div className="ctrl-row"> <div className="ctrl"><label>Market drift: <b id="gr-tr-v">ranging</b></label><input type="range" id="gr-tr" min="-10" max="10" defaultValue="0" /></div> <button className="btn" id="gr-reset">Restart</button> </div> <div className="readout"> <div className="r"><span className="k">Rungs banked</span><span className="v pos" id="gr-rungs">0</span></div> <div className="r"><span className="k">Rung P&amp;L</span><span className="v pos" id="gr-pnl">0.0</span></div> <div className="r"><span className="k">Open inventory</span><span className="v" id="gr-inv">0</span></div> <div className="r"><span className="k">Inventory MTM</span><span className="v" id="gr-mtm">0.0</span></div> <div className="r"><span className="k">Total</span><span className="v" id="gr-tot">0.0</span></div> </div> </div> <div className="fig-cap">Green rungs are resting buys, red rungs resting sells; each completed buy-then-sell flashes and banks one rung of profit. At drift = 0 (ranging) the machine hums: rung P&amp;L climbs, inventory oscillates near zero. Now drag the drift negative and watch the pathology: price slides down the ladder filling <em>every buy on the way</em>, no sell ever completes, and the "profit per rung" line is dwarfed by the red mark-to-market of a growing long position. That is the Martingale trap — the grid quietly converts into a maximum-size bet that the trend will reverse. This is why every serious grid runs a hard stop beyond the last rung.</div> </div> <div className="proscons"><div className="p">+ Dead simple; shines in sideways chop; psychologically easy to operate.</div><div className="c">− In a trend it Martingales into the falling side; the "profit per rung" hides a short-trend position. Needs hard stop-loss beyond the grid.</div></div></div>

<div className="algo"><h4>5 · Cross-exchange hedged MM</h4><div className="fit">FIT: crypto long tail; any quote-here-hedge-there pair of venues</div> <p>Quote where the spread is wide; on every fill, hedge instantly where the book is deep. Inventory half-life drops from hours to seconds, and the P&amp;L of each round trip becomes an arithmetic check you can audit daily:</p> <div className="eq"><span className="lbl">Cross-exchange round trip</span> net = quoted spread − maker fee − taker fee − hedge slippage <span className="where"><span className="wt">What the symbols mean</span> <span className="sym">quoted spread</span> — the wide spread you capture on the quiet venue &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">maker fee</span> — what the quiet venue charges your resting quote &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">taker fee</span> — what the deep venue charges the hedge order (it crosses the spread, so it takes) &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">hedge slippage</span> — how far the deep venue's price moves against you between your fill and your hedge landing; it grows with your latency. If net &gt; 0 across your fills, the business exists.</span></div> <div className="fig"> <div className="fig-bar"><span className="dot d1"></span><span className="dot d2"></span><span className="dot d3"></span><span className="fig-t">animation — fill here, hedge there</span></div> <div className="fig-body"> <canvas id="cv-xmm" height="230"></canvas> <div className="ctrl-row"> <div className="ctrl"><label>Hedge latency: <b id="xm-lat-v">80ms</b></label><input type="range" id="xm-lat" min="1" max="30" defaultValue="8" /></div> </div> <div className="readout"> <div className="r"><span className="k">Round trips</span><span className="v" id="xm-n">0</span></div> <div className="r"><span className="k">Avg spread captured</span><span className="v pos" id="xm-spr">—</span></div> <div className="r"><span className="k">Avg slippage + fees</span><span className="v neg" id="xm-slip">—</span></div> <div className="r"><span className="k">Net P&amp;L</span><span className="v" id="xm-net">0.0</span></div> </div> </div> <div className="fig-cap">Left book: the quiet long-tail venue where you quote a wide spread. Right book: the deep venue you hedge into. When a trader hits your quote (flash), a hedge order streaks across to the deep book after your latency delay — and the deep price keeps moving while it flies. At low latency the hedge lands near your fill price and nearly all the spread survives as profit. Drag latency up and watch the slippage bar eat the spread: the inventory risk you engineered away has been traded for <strong>latency risk</strong>, and this slider is its price.</div> </div> <div className="proscons"><div className="p">+ Near-market-neutral; inventory risk mostly engineered away.</div><div className="c">− Latency between venues is the new risk; balance/transfer management; doubles your fee load.</div></div> <p className="small">Reference implementation: Hummingbot's legacy <code>cross_exchange_market_making</code> strategy (V2 controller: <code>xemm_multiple_levels</code>) — set the maker venue, the taker venue, and <code>min_profitability</code>, and it enforces the inequality above for you.</p></div>

<div className="algo"><h4>6 · Microprice-anchored quoting</h4><div className="fit">FIT: any CLOB with trustworthy L2 depth</div> <p>Replace the mid with Stoikov's microprice (or weighted mid) as the quoting anchor, leaning away from the side the book says is about to break. Cheap, measurable adverse-selection reduction; combines with #2/#3 (anchor swap) rather than competing with them.</p> <div className="proscons"><div className="p">+ Free markout improvement; trivially composable.</div><div className="c">− Imbalance can be spoofed; needs clean, fast book data.</div></div></div>

<div className="algo"><h4>7 · Reinforcement-learning MM</h4><div className="fit">FIT: research frontier; safest as a parameter-tuner</div> <p>Frame quoting as a game the machine plays millions of times — a <em>Markov decision process</em>:</p> <div className="eq"><span className="lbl">The RL loop, in words</span> state → action → reward → (better policy) → state → … <span className="where"><span className="wt">What the pieces mean</span> <span className="sym">state</span> — what the bot can see right now: its inventory, the book imbalance, recent volatility, any signals &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">action</span> — what it can do: choose a spread and a skew (or, safer, choose the γ and k of an AS/GLFT engine) &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">reward</span> — the score it's trying to maximize: risk-adjusted P&amp;L, typically profit minus a penalty on held inventory &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">policy</span> — the learned rulebook mapping every state to an action. Training = replaying markets until the rulebook stops improving.</span></div> <p>What does a trained policy actually look like? Not magic — a lookup table with structure. Watch one emerge:</p> <div className="fig"> <div className="fig-bar"><span className="dot d1"></span><span className="dot d2"></span><span className="dot d3"></span><span className="fig-t">interactive — watch a quoting policy get learned</span></div> <div className="fig-body"> <canvas id="cv-rl" height="240"></canvas> <div className="ctrl-row"> <div className="ctrl"><label>Training progress: <b id="rl-t-v">0%</b></label><input type="range" id="rl-t" min="0" max="100" defaultValue="0" /></div> </div> <div className="readout"> <div className="r"><span className="k">Policy at (long, buyers queuing)</span><span className="v" id="rl-a">—</span></div> <div className="r"><span className="k">Reading</span><span className="v" id="rl-read" style={{fontSize:'13px',maxWidth:'360px'}}>—</span></div> </div> </div> <div className="fig-cap">Each cell is a market state — inventory across, book imbalance down — and its color is the action the policy has learned there (green = skew quotes up / lean to buy, red = skew down / lean to sell, pale = quote symmetric). At 0% training the map is noise: the agent acts randomly. Drag training forward and structure crystallizes: long inventory ⇒ skew down to shed it; heavy bid-side imbalance ⇒ lean with the coming move. By 100% the machine has rediscovered, cell by cell, roughly what Avellaneda–Stoikov wrote as one formula — which is the deepest lesson of RL market making: <em>when it works, it mostly re-learns the textbook, plus nonlinear corrections at the edges</em>.</div> </div> <p>The honest reading of the literature, mid-2026: the classic pattern is <strong>Alpha-AS</strong> (Falces Marin et al. 2022) — let RL choose the <em>parameters</em> of an analytical AS engine rather than raw quotes, keeping a safety skeleton. Since then the frontier has moved on three fronts: <em>robustness</em> (adversarial training — Spooner &amp; Savani 2020 — and non-stationary, clustered order-flow models), <em>better simulators</em> (generative "world models" of the order book — token-level autoregressive flow models, diffusion engines like TRADES, GPU-parallel books like JAX-LOB — because an RL agent is only as honest as the market it trained in), and <em>theory</em> (online learning of AS's fill parameters with provable logarithmic regret, Cao et al. 2024). Most results still live in simulators; regime shift and your own market impact remain the unsolved parts. There is, so far, no "GPT moment" for market making.</p> <div className="proscons"><div className="p">+ Adapts nonlinearly; ingests arbitrary signals; Alpha-AS pattern keeps an analytical safety skeleton.</div><div className="c">− Sample-hungry; simulator-to-live gap; uninterpretable failures. Never deploy unbounded.</div></div></div>

<div className="algo"><h4>8 · Alpha-anchored PMM (signal-driven reference price)</h4><div className="fit">FIT: crypto perps and any venue where you can mine your own signal</div> <p>Pure market making with one upgrade: the <em>reference price</em> you quote around is shifted by a short-horizon predictive signal, so the whole quote ladder leans toward where price is about to go. Hummingbot's <code>pmm_dynamic</code> controller does this with MACD; Stoikov's Cornell group showed the anchor is nearly interchangeable — and that a one-line candlestick feature they call <strong>Bar Portion</strong> beat MACD in live trading (Chapter 17 turns their pipeline into a full workflow). It's the retail-scale cousin of entry #6: pick a better center, and every fill gets a little less toxic.</p> <div className="proscons"><div className="p">+ Simple to bolt onto any PMM; signal quality is measurable offline (quintile analysis) before risking a cent.</div><div className="c">− Signals decay as others find them; a wrong signal is worse than none — it leans you <em>into</em> the move.</div></div></div>

<div className="keyidea"><span className="tag">The composition insight</span>These aren't eight rivals; they're modules. Microprice or alpha anchor (6, 8) + GLFT skeleton (3) + cross-exchange hedge (5) + RL-tuned parameters (7) is one coherent engine — and is, in fact, Proposal F.</div>
