### The mechanics that matter

<ul> <li><strong>One book, two views.</strong> The book stores YES bids and NO bids; a YES bid at 7¢ <em>is</em> a NO ask at 93¢. "Buying NO" and "selling YES" are the same act.</li> <li><strong>Prices are probabilities.</strong> 1¢–99¢, tick = 1¢ (with finer sub-penny structures rolling out via <code>price_ranges[].step</code>).</li> <li><strong>MM-friendly API.</strong> Amend Order (keep the order's identity; note the fine print — <em>only a size decrease preserves your FIFO spot; a reprice goes to the back of the queue</em>, Ch. 7), batch create/cancel, queue-position endpoint (you can <em>see</em> your FIFO position), self-trade prevention modes (<code>taker_at_cross</code> / <code>maker</code>), <code>cancel_order_on_pause</code>. These exist because Kalshi wants makers.</li> <li><strong>Idle cash earns interest</strong> (3.25% APY, variable — and it accrues on open-position collateral too), softening the cost of parked capital.</li> </ul>

### Why quote only the 30–70¢ band?

<ul> <li><strong>Tail asymmetry:</strong> sell a 95¢ YES that resolves against you and you lose 95¢ to win 5¢ — one informed counterparty erases 19 good trades.</li> <li><strong>Tick coarseness:</strong> at 3¢, one tick is 33% of the price; spreads can't compress to fair levels.</li> <li><strong>Flow quality:</strong> the center is where genuine two-way uncertainty (and noise flow) lives; the tails attract precisely the flow that knows something.</li> </ul>

<div className="keyidea"><span className="tag">Kalshi MM doctrine</span>Maker-only, center-band, model-anchored, queue-aware (shrink in place when you can; accept that any reprice pays the back-of-queue toll), with hard inventory caps per market. The venue's API was practically designed for this playbook. (One 2026 footnote: Kalshi now also lists perpetual futures — everything in this chapter is about its event contracts.)</div>
