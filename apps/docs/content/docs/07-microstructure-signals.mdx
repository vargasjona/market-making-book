### Signal 1 — The microprice (Stoikov, 2018)

<p>The mid-price treats the book as symmetric. It usually isn't. If 900 contracts rest on the bid and 100 on the ask, the next move is more likely up — buyers are queuing, sellers are scarce. Stoikov's <strong>microprice</strong> formalizes this: it is the martingale (unbiased) estimator of the near-future price given book imbalance and spread, computed from a Markov model of book states. A first-order cousin you can compute instantly is the imbalance-weighted mid:</p>

<div className="eq"><span className="lbl">Weighted mid (microprice's simple cousin)</span> <i>P</i><sub>w</sub> = ( <i>P</i><sub>ask</sub> · <i>Q</i><sub>bid</sub> + <i>P</i><sub>bid</sub> · <i>Q</i><sub>ask</sub> ) / ( <i>Q</i><sub>bid</sub> + <i>Q</i><sub>ask</sub> ) <span className="where"><span className="wt">What the symbols mean</span> <span className="sym"><i>P</i></span> is a <em>price</em>, <span className="sym"><i>Q</i></span> is a <em>quantity</em> (size). Specifically: <span className="sym"><i>P</i><sub>bid</sub></span> — the best (highest) price buyers are offering &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym"><i>P</i><sub>ask</sub></span> — the best (lowest) price sellers are asking &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym"><i>Q</i><sub>bid</sub></span> — how many contracts are resting at that best bid &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym"><i>Q</i><sub>ask</sub></span> — how many are resting at the best ask &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym"><i>P</i><sub>w</sub></span> — the result: a weighted average of the two prices that leans toward whichever side of the book is <em>thinner</em>.</span></div>

<p>Note the cross: heavy <em>bid</em> size pulls the estimate toward the <em>ask</em> — each price is weighted by the <em>opposite</em> side's quantity, which is what makes the estimate lean toward the side about to give way. Worked example: bid 48¢ with 900 resting, ask 50¢ with 100 resting → <i>P</i><sub>w</sub> = (50·900 + 48·100)/(900+100) = 49.8¢, almost at the ask, while the plain mid says 49¢. Anchoring your quotes on the microprice instead of the mid means you lean away from the side that's about to get run over — a free reduction in adverse selection.</p>

### Signal 2 — Queue position

<p>Under price-time priority, being first at a price level is worth real money: the early order collects the benign, random fills, while the back of the queue gets filled mostly when the level is about to be swept through — i.e., precisely when you don't want to be filled. Practical consequences: cancel-and-replace resets you to the back — and so does any <em>reprice</em>, even through an amend endpoint. What an amend buys you (Kalshi's Amend Order — Chapter 9) is keeping the order's identity, and crucially: <strong>shrinking size in place keeps your spot in the queue</strong>, while moving price (or growing size) forfeits it everywhere. So treat queue position as an asset you spend, not a free action — the cheapest risk reduction is often cutting size at the same price rather than repricing. On large-tick instruments (where the spread is almost always one tick), queue position <em>is</em> the whole game.</p>

### Signal 3 — Flow toxicity (VPIN)

<p>Easley, López de Prado and O'Hara's <strong>VPIN</strong> (Volume-Synchronized Probability of Informed Trading) estimates, in near-real-time, how informed the current flow is: chop volume into equal-size buckets, classify each bucket's volume as buyer- or seller-initiated, and track the absolute imbalance. Persistent one-sided pressure ⇒ rising VPIN ⇒ the Glosten–Milgrom μ of Chapter 5 is climbing. VPIN famously spiked in the hour before the 2010 Flash Crash (though its predictive power is academically contested — Andersen and Bondarenko argue it mostly tracks trading intensity). Use it as a <strong>regime alarm</strong>, not an oracle: when toxicity rises, widen or pull; when it normalizes, re-tighten.</p>

### Fees: the fourth signal that isn't optional

<p>None of these signals matter if the fee math doesn't close. The viability inequality of all market making:</p>

<div className="eq"><span className="lbl">The viability condition</span> E[captured spread] + rebates &nbsp;&gt;&nbsp; E[adverse selection loss] + fees + hedging costs <span className="where"><span className="wt">What the symbols mean</span> <span className="sym">E[·]</span> — expected (average) value over many trades, as in Chapter 3 &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">captured spread</span> — what your round trips actually earn, not the quoted spread &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">rebates</span> — money some venues pay <em>you</em> for providing liquidity as a maker &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">adverse selection loss</span> — the average cost of fills that arrive right before the price moves against you &nbsp;·&nbsp; <span className="sym">fees, hedging costs</span> — what the venue charges, plus what you pay elsewhere to lay off risk. If the left side doesn't beat the right side <em>on average</em>, no algorithm can save the strategy.</span></div>

<p>Maker rebates push the left side up; taker fees on your hedges push the right side up. Part III evaluates each venue with exactly this inequality.</p>
